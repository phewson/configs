For missing driver age, the mixture likelihood is:
$p(y) = \sum_{a=1}^A w_a p(y|a)

with mixture weights $w_a$​ satisfying:
$\sum_a w_a = 1, $w_a geq 0$

If the weights are proportional to $\exp(\eta_a)$:

$w_a = \frac{\exp(\eta_a)}{\sum_{a'} \exp(\eta_{a'})},$ i.e., the softmax.

In stan we do:

lp_mix[a] = eta[a] + log p(y | a);
then
target += log_sum_exp(lp_mix);
i.e., $\log \sum_a exp(\eta_a + \log(p(y|a))$, or  $\log \sum_a exp(\eta_a) p(y|a)$
Factor out the softmax denominator

$=\log \left( \sum_a \exp(\eta_a) \right) + \log \sum_a \frac{\exp(\eta_a)}{\sum_a' \exp(\eta_a')} p(y|a)$
$\log \sum_a exp(\eta_a) + \log(\sum_a w_a p(y|a))$ which differs from the trus mixture likelihood only by an additive constant.



The first naive attempt tried

Complete (a > 0, v > 0)  | mu_surface[a,v]       | Very specific cell-level rate                  
 Missing driver age        | mu_sum_over_a[v] | Total over all driver ages at fixed vehicle age
Missing vehicle age     | mu_sum_over_v[a]  |Total over all vehicle ages at fixed driver age

That may be too crude, sum over driver age isn't necessarily the correct answer.  A missing driver age means that this count could have come from any driver age, with equal prior probability, weighted only by the Poisson mean in that age.  If the missingness is informative, then marginalising over all ages is too crude and will systematically bias the column/row sums.  I worry about this "missingness is informative".

So, instead of assuming uniform prior over the ages, we need to introduce *latent categorical variables*. This prior should depend on some covariate. 
$P(age=a | v, missing) \propto exp(η_surface[a,v])$  This makes the predictive mean mu_marginal[v] = sum_a [ mu[a,v] * softmax(η_surface[,v])[a] ].  This (apparently) allows the model to infer which ages plausibly generated the missing entry.

OR, we could treat the missing ages as latent discrete parameters. Stan cannot sample discrete directly, but we could marginalise them as mixtures at the likelihood level.
target += log_sum_exp( log_mu[a,v]  for all a consistent with missingness ). This is what we do, but we need to introduce some kind of data-driven weights.

poisson_lpmf(y | mu_sum) implies that if a count had missing driver age, we assume it equals: "a Poisson observation of the sum of all cell means" But another plausible generative mechanism is: "one unobserved cell produces the count, but we don’t know which".

 ## Offset priors for missing cells (“structural priors”)
The generative probability of an age depends on its underlying exposure or expected counts.

So instead of a flat prior, we use:
log_prior_driver[a] = log_pop[a, v];   // or row totals
or
log_prior_driver[a] = eta_surface[a,v];   // softmax of the smooth surface
This gives us:
lp_mix = log_prior_driver[a]
       + poisson_log_lpmf(y[i] | log_mu[a, v]);

Count data modelling is fundamental in various fields (epidemiology, demography, transportation safety).   2d splines might reveal patterns.   However, missing labels (unobserved covariates due to incomplete recording, aggregation or structural gaps in contingency tables). Complete-case analysis can lead to biased estimates.

Marginalisation over missing values offers a principled alternative, integrating out unobserved components by summing Poisson probabilities across margins to derive likelihood for observed aggregates. This preserves the Poisson mean-variance relationship and avoids ad-hoc imputation. However, implementing in spline models requires careful handling, often via mixture representations where the observed count is a finite mixture of latent Poisson components weighted by marginal probabilities.  The validity of this strategy (i.e., consistency, bias reduction and robustness under model mis-specficiation) remain underexplored.  This is particularly true of 2d splines where smoothness penalties interact with incomplete data structures. Prior work has advanced mixture models for overdispersed counts and Bayesian inference for flexible distributions. Synthesis across these strands is rare.

1. Marginalisation for missing labels in poisson models.

   Marginalisation over missing covariate labels or categories in Poisson count models involves summing Poisson probabilities across unobserved margins to derive the observed likelihood, enabling full maximum likelihood estimation without imputation. This approach assumes MAR and indepdendence of latent counts, yielding ubniased estimates of incidence densities and risk rations e.g. Ryan et al 1999.  In spline contexts, 2d P splines integrte this by treating unobserved age-year cells as latent, summing contributions to fit smoothed surfaces with penalties on second differences, achieving dimensions of 10-20 parameters for mortality trends (Camarda 2012). Peres-Ruiz and escarela 2018.  It is computationally slow Shyamalkumar 2005.
